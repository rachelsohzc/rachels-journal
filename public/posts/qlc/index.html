5 things I learned from my Quarter-Life Crisis

Sunday, Jul 21, 2024 | 4 minutes read | Update at Sunday, Jul 21, 2024

@ rachel

I turn 24 this year, and I’d like to say that I am almost at level 3 (aka year 3) of this game called adulthood. I’ve weathered through my first quarter-life crisis. I still don’t have everything figured out, but I want to share my story.

HOW IT STARTED

I started my first job away from London, where I did my Bachelor’s. When I landed my job, you could say I shed blood, sweat, and tears because I thought this was the path to my perfect life. A few months in, I switched gears to autopilot because I felt so numb every day. I couldn’t express how I felt in words. Instead, there would be random nights where I would burst into tears without knowing why.

I reached my breaking point after a series of events.

It started with a road trip to Switzerland with my friends. We had a bit of a road accident, and my friend struck something in me when he asked if I was okay. I was confused because I thought I was perfectly fine, but he mentioned that he had never before seen me so desensitised to my surroundings.

Not long after Switzerland, I had to deal with an end to a situationship.

Going through all the emotions, I think one common factor kept popping up. I was avoiding something - I suppressed my frustrations when I did things that weren’t in tune with what I truly wanted to do in life. I didn’t tell my friends and family what I was going through and insisted that this was my problem alone to fix. I avoided being alone with my thoughts as much as I could.

Even though I couldn’t pinpoint what was wrong, I knew one thing - I was extremely unhappy with my life.

SELF-GROWTH

I want to use this quote my dad always tells me when I come to him with life problems before talking about what I’ve learned:

“Happiness takes a lot of effort. No one is magically happy. You have a choice - either put in the effort and live a meaningful life, or take the easy way out and wonder about the what ifs when you’re on your death bed.”

None of these lessons would’ve mattered if I wasn’t willing to put in the work.

Lesson 1: Listen to your emotions

Ignoring my emotions made me switch to living life on autopilot. Often, the more we suppress our feelings, the more they try to be heard.

I wanted to document my life, whether it was through videos, blogs, or pictures. But I didn’t because I was worried I would look like a cringy influencer. I started to plan for the future as if I wanted to settle down and buy a house to live in for the rest of my life with a partner. It’s something society often expects you to do. But I don’t even want to do that. The very thought of that makes me squirmish.

Acknowledge those feelings, even if they’re unconventional. They’ll lead and guide you to what you want to do.

Lesson 2: Have uncomfortable conversations

Uncomfortable conversations include ugly thoughts and admitting things you don’t want to believe about yourself.

For a couple of months, whenever I felt frustrated or upset, I would get out my phone and record myself talking. I would then watch back those videos and pretend to be a third party watching this girl talk about her life. As I listened to this girl talk about her life, I wondered why she kept crying about her dissatisfaction with her job role and loneliness as if she was powerless to change things. That’s when I realised that I had gotten comfortable acting like a victim in any unfortunate scenario I found myself in.

From there, I started actively seeking projects that interested me at work, forced myself to go to dance classes despite being scared (I’ve wanted to dance for ages, but I’ve always been worried about looking stiff and stupid), and became the friend who would reach out to organise things like board games night.

Admitting that you’re a blocker to your own goals can make you more open to change because once you’ve realised your mistakes, the only next step is “How can I improve and do better for myself?”
